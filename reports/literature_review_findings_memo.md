## Strongest established findings

1. **Hong Kong AMI historically cold-dominant (daily data).** Goggins et al. (2013): below ~24°C, ~3.7% higher AMI admissions per 1°C decrease (lags 0–13); no significant heat effect in that study; NO₂ important.
2. **Hong Kong stroke historically cold-related, subtype-specific (daily data).** Goggins et al. (2012): hemorrhagic stroke showed a strong inverse temperature association (lags 0–4); ischemic stroke weaker and mainly below ~22°C (lags 0–13); pollutants not associated.
3. **Warm-climate winter excess is plausible.** Sipilä (Finland seasonality) and Lorking (Thailand winter excess) support seasonal stroke burdens outside classic temperate heat narratives.
4. **Administrative principal inpatient diagnoses can have high PPV** for AMI/IS in East Asian validation studies (Korea NHIS 2024; Tianjin 2025), supporting insistence on principal diagnosis.

## Defensible remaining contribution

A restrained novelty statement:

> Among Hong Kong residents aged 35+, estimate **monthly** associations of **officially defined** thermal extremes (especially hot nights and cold days) with **principal-diagnosis inpatient** AMI, ischemic stroke, and hemorrhagic stroke during **2013–2023**, with age-sex population offsets, separate older-age bands (including 65–69 and 70–74), COVID handling, and staged pollution adjustment — without assuming a heat-for-cold regime shift.

This remains defensible because recent HK hot-night work is largely **daily**, often **hot-season**, and not focused on **monthly official extreme counts × diagnosis-specific principal inpatient admissions** with the proposed demographic offsets. It is **not** defensible to claim the first Hong Kong temperature–CVD study, nor the first hot-night hospitalization study.

## Claims that should be removed or weakened

- “Little is known about hot nights and cardiovascular admissions in Hong Kong.” → Too strong after Guo 2024.
- “We will prove a regime shift from cold to heat.” → Remove.
- Direct numeric comparison of our monthly RRs with Goggins per-°C daily estimates → Avoid.
- Treating official binary hot nights as obviously superior without acknowledging Guo’s intensity finding → Soften; keep binary as primary for policy alignment, with intensity/spell metrics as alternatives.

## Recommended primary hypotheses (prespecify)

1. Cold days are positively associated with monthly AMI and ischemic stroke admission rates.
2. Hot nights may be associated with admission rates, possibly more detectable in older age groups; null findings remain interpretable given monthly aggregation.
3. Associations differ by diagnosis (AMI vs IS vs HS).
4. Ozone adjustment may attenuate heat associations if ozone lies partly on the pathway.

## Recommended sensitivity analyses

Exclude 2020–2022; pre-COVID only; lag-1 month; very hot days instead of hot nights; spell metrics / intensity-style alternatives if constructible monthly; single-pollutant then multi-pollutant; principal-diagnosis alternatives if coding confirmed.
