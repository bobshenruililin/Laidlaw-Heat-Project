# Introduction (draft)

**Working title:** Thermal extremes and cardiovascular hospital burden in an aging Hong Kong, 2013–2023  

*Draft for internal review. Ecological associations only; no causal claims. Outcome analysis awaits Hospital Authority data.*

---

Cardiovascular disease remains a leading cause of hospitalization and death worldwide, and ambient temperature is an established environmental correlate of acute cardiac and cerebrovascular events. Both cold and heat can stress thermoregulatory and cardiovascular systems through changes in blood pressure, viscosity, autonomic tone, and inflammatory pathways. In temperate settings, heat waves have received substantial attention. In subtropical cities, however, cooler weather has often been linked to larger seasonal burdens of ischemic heart disease and stroke, even when absolute winter temperatures are milder than those of colder climates.

Hong Kong is a dense, humid, aging subtropical city in which housing stock, urban heat-island conditions, and high-rise living shape exposure to both daytime heat and overnight warmth. The Hong Kong Observatory defines operational thermal extremes that are directly relevant to public communication and preparedness: hot nights (daily minimum temperature ≥28°C), very hot days (daily maximum ≥33°C), extremely hot days (daily maximum ≥35°C), and cold days (daily minimum ≤12°C). Hot nights are of particular interest because overnight heat may reduce physiological recovery, especially among older adults and residents of poorly cooled dwellings. At the same time, cold days remain part of the local warning system and may continue to matter for cardiovascular admissions.

Historical Hong Kong evidence, including work by Goggins and colleagues on acute myocardial infarction and related analyses of stroke and winter excess morbidity, has generally pointed toward a cold-sensitive cardiovascular hospitalization profile in earlier periods, with limited evidence of strong heat effects and with air pollution—particularly nitrogen dioxide in some AMI analyses—also contributing to short-term risk. Those studies largely used daily time-series designs and earlier decades. Whether the contemporary pattern is similar, weaker, or accompanied by a more detectable heat-related signal remains an open empirical question.

Several contextual changes make 2013–2023 a useful reassessment window. First, observed thermal extremes have shifted: in the processed Hong Kong Observatory Headquarters series for this project, annual hot nights and very hot days were substantially higher in 2019–2023 than in 2013–2018, while cold-day counts remained variable rather than simply disappearing. Second, Hong Kong’s population is aging, with growth concentrated in older age bands that are clinically vulnerable and operationally important for hospital planning; separating ages 65–69 and 70–74 is therefore scientifically and practically useful if data allow. Third, air-pollution profiles have changed under clean-air policies, with declines in some particulate and nitrogen dioxide concentrations and continued concern about ozone, which may correlate with hot sunny conditions and should not be treated automatically as a conventional confounder. Fourth, COVID-19 disrupted care-seeking, hospital capacity, and infection patterns during 2020–2022, requiring explicit handling in any admission time series.

This study is designed as a retrospective ecological time-series analysis of monthly Hospital Authority cardiovascular admissions among residents aged 35 years and older. The primary aim is to assess whether monthly counts of officially defined thermal extremes—especially hot nights and cold days—are associated with admission rates for acute myocardial infarction, ischemic stroke, and hemorrhagic stroke, and whether associations vary by age, sex, diagnosis, pollutant environment, and COVID period. Monthly aggregation is accepted as a constraint: it can capture multi-day and seasonal burden but may attenuate acute short-lag heat effects; therefore extreme-day counts and spell metrics are preferred over monthly mean temperature alone.

Importantly, the study does **not** begin from a claim that climate change has already shifted Hong Kong’s cardiovascular weather-risk profile from cold-dominant to heat-dominant. A cold-dominant contemporary result would remain scientifically and policy-relevant for hospital preparedness. The contribution of the present work is to provide a transparent, pre-specified reassessment under recent climatic, demographic, and pollution conditions, using population denominators and staged environmental adjustment, once approved Hospital Authority outcome data are available.

**Study aim (cautious):** To estimate associations between monthly thermal extremes and cardiovascular hospital admission rates in Hong Kong from 2013–2023, and to evaluate whether contemporary patterns are consistent with, or differ from, the historically cold-dominant profile reported in earlier local studies—without asserting individual-level causal effects.
