## 1. Temperature and acute myocardial infarction

Daily time-series studies in many settings report U- or J-shaped temperature–AMI relationships, with cold often producing larger and more prolonged risks than heat. In Hong Kong, Goggins et al. (2013) analyzed daily AMI admissions (2000–2009) and found that cooling below an estimated ~24°C threshold was associated with higher AMI admissions (~3.7% per 1°C using multi-day lags), without a statistically significant heat effect in that study period; same-day NO₂ was the strongest pollutant predictor among those examined. A later Hong Kong DLNM analysis stratified by diabetes status (Lam et al., 2018) found stronger cold-season AMI associations among patients with diabetes and some hot-season sensitivity in that subgroup. These findings motivate retaining cold as a co-primary exposure and treating heat as an open empirical question rather than a presumed replacement for cold.

## 2. Temperature and ischemic stroke

Evidence for ischemic stroke is more heterogeneous across climates and lag structures. In Hong Kong, Goggins et al. (2012) reported a weaker ischemic-stroke association that was apparent mainly below ~22°C with longer lags (0–13). A recent weekly Hong Kong analysis (1998–2019) again linked colder weather—and influenza activity—to stroke admissions, reinforcing cold and infection pathways, though weekly pooled stroke endpoints limit subtype-specific inference. Outside Hong Kong, seasonal studies in both temperate (Sipilä et al., 2017) and tropical (Lorking et al., 2020) settings show winter or autumn excesses, supporting the biological and operational relevance of cooler periods even where absolute temperatures are mild.

## 3. Temperature and hemorrhagic stroke

Hemorrhagic stroke often shows different seasonality and temperature sensitivity from ischemic stroke. In the Hong Kong daily analysis, hemorrhagic stroke exhibited a strong, approximately linear inverse association with mean temperature across the observed range (lags 0–4), stronger among older adults and women (Goggins et al., 2012). This supports modeling hemorrhagic stroke separately and avoiding pooled “stroke” endpoints as the primary analysis.

## 4. Hong Kong-specific evidence and recent overlap

Hong Kong already has a substantial temperature–health literature. Beyond the Goggins AMI and stroke papers, recent daily studies examine nighttime heat and hospitalization (Guo et al., 2024), temperature–pollution modification of emergency admissions (Guo et al., 2025), and cold/influenza associations with stroke (Wang et al., 2025). Critically, Guo et al. (2024) found that **official binary hot nights (Tmin ≥28°C) were not associated with excess hospitalization risk**, whereas a measure of nighttime heat **intensity** (hot-night excess) was associated with hospitalization, including circulatory admissions, especially among older and socioeconomically disadvantaged groups. Therefore the present project must not claim to be the first Hong Kong hot-night hospitalization study. Its contribution must be framed around monthly official extreme counts, diagnosis-specific principal inpatient admissions, demographic offsets, and COVID-period handling in 2013–2023.

## 5. Hot nights, humid heat, and heat-spell duration

Hot nights are policy-relevant in Hong Kong’s warning system and biologically plausible because overnight heat may impair recovery. Spell duration and intensity metrics may capture cumulative stress better than binary day counts. Mortality studies of consecutive hot days/nights in Hong Kong support event-based extremes as informative exposures. For a monthly admissions design, monthly hot-night counts and spell-length summaries are reasonable primary/alternative metrics, but Guo’s intensity finding implies that null associations for binary hot nights would not close the scientific question.

## 6. Age and sex as modifiers

Older adults consistently show greater cold- and heat-related vulnerability in Hong Kong and elsewhere. Separating ages 65–69 and 70–74, and retaining 75–84 and 85+, is justified by demographic growth and clinical risk. Sex differences appear in some stroke analyses (e.g., stronger hemorrhagic associations among women in Goggins 2012). Age–sex population offsets are essential so demographic aging is not mistaken for environmental effects.

## 7. Air pollution and temperature

NO₂ was important in earlier AMI work; contemporary Hong Kong evidence shows declining some pollutants alongside ongoing ozone concern, and recent work shows pollution can amplify temperature–hospitalization associations. Ozone is particularly problematic as a “standard confounder” because it correlates with hot sunny conditions and may partly mediate heat effects. Staged models (none → single pollutant → interaction → multi-pollutant sensitivity) are therefore preferred.

## 8. Monthly versus daily time-series designs

Daily DLNMs estimate acute triggering over specific lags. Monthly models estimate broader hospital-burden associations and can miss short-lag heat spikes. Extreme-day counts improve monthly exposure description but do not restore daily lag resolution. Historical per-°C daily estimates therefore cannot be compared directly with monthly rate ratios per five hot nights or cold days.

## 9. Remaining gap and implications for the proposed study

After accounting for recent Hong Kong studies, a defensible gap remains: a transparent, prespecified assessment of whether **monthly official thermal extremes**—especially hot nights and cold days—are associated with **principal-diagnosis inpatient AMI and stroke subtypes** in an aging Hong Kong during 2013–2023, with population offsets, COVID handling, and staged pollution adjustment. The project should not assume a regime shift; a cold-dominant result would remain important. Binary hot-night findings should be interpreted alongside intensity/spell alternatives and the known limitations of monthly aggregation.
