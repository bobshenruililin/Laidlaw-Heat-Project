## How a cold ≫ heat AF could relate to the recalled null pattern

If the confirmed source reports a much larger cold-attributable fraction than heat-attributable fraction, that could be **compatible** with fewer non-significant cold-side tests: a larger or more persistent signal may be estimated more precisely under a fixed, comparable test set. It does not establish the recalled pattern.

AF magnitude and p-value frequency answer different questions. AF depends on the exposure distribution, reference temperature, lag structure, model and outcome burden; a p-value also depends on standard error, sample size, contrast choice and multiplicity. Unequal hot/cold definitions or numbers of tests can create different “null” proportions even when AFs are not directly comparable. Conversely, a large AF can still have a wide interval, and p ≥ 0.05 does not demonstrate no effect.

Therefore, first identify the paper and reconstruct its complete test universe. Report estimates and confidence intervals as primary; treat counts of p-values above or below 0.05 as a secondary, source-matched description only.
